    term: Explosion

      Explosion-related technological incidents can be defined as accidental or
      intentional rapid energetic events that result in the actual or potential
      exposure of responders and/or members of the public to a chemical hazard
      (adapted from WHO, no date).
    source:

    description: >-
      The effects of explosions can be devastating in terms of lives lost,
      injuries, damage to property and the environment, and to business
      continuity. Working with flammable liquids, dusts, gases and solids can be
      hazardous because of the risk of explosion and potentially a subsequent
      fire (HSE, no date). Explosion effects can include blast waves, fragment
      and debris throw, cratering and ground shock (USNRC, 2013).

      Explosions can result from accidents at hazardous installations (such as
      chemical plants) and accidents while hazardous substances are in transport
      (such as by tankers or lorries). Notable examples of industrial explosions
      include those at the Piper Alpha oil platform in the North Sea in 1986
      (Cullen, 1990), the ammonium nitrate (c.f. HIP CH0902) explosion in Beirut
      Lebanon in 2020 (Al-Hajj et al., 2021; El Sayed, 2020), the AZF fertiliser
      factory in Toulouse, France in 2001 (French Ministry of Sustainable
      Development, 2013) and the Buncefield oil storage depot in 2005 (HSE,
      2011). Fires and explosions are frequently grouped together but an
      explosion can happen without a fire.

      Explosion-related technological incidents can suddenly and overtly release
      hazardous chemicals (e.g. toxic gases (CH0300), persistent organic
      pollutants (CH0500), strong acids and bases (CH0901)) into the
      environment. The factors leading up to an incident can include poor
      maintenance of manufacturing and storage equipment, lack of regulation
      and/or poor enforcement of safety regulations, road traffic accidents,
      human error, natural events such as heavy rain, earthquakes, hurricanes,
      floods, and terrorism (WHO, no date).

      Most explosion-related technological incidents occur at the interfaces
      between the transport, storage, processing, use, and disposal of hazardous
      chemicals, where these systems are more vulnerable to failure, error or
      manipulation. Exposure levels generally differ for the different people
      involved in a chemical incident (WHO, no date):

      - Employees and other on-site persons: usually more than one exposure
      pathway, often inhalation (breathing) of smoke and vapours and skin
      contact from splashing and clean-up of chemicals.

      - Emergency services: usually close to the emergency and involved in
      rescue, containment of chemicals, managing the impact of the explosion and
      extinguishing fires; primary and secondary contamination of fire officers,
      ambulance officers, and other emergency staff; secondary contamination of
      medical staff and other hospital patients of incomplete decontamination of
      causalities.

      - Public: exposure via air, water, food, soil etc.
